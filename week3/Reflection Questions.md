# Reflection Questions: 

**1. Why does flocking emerge?**

It happens naturally when lots of individuals follow simple, local rules—like steering away from neighbors, matching their speed, and staying close. When everyone does this at once, the whole group moves together organically.

**2. Why is no leader required?**

There's no "boss" because everyone just pays attention to their immediate neighbors. This local information ripples through the group, creating coordinated behavior without anyone giving orders.

**3. What happens when perception radius becomes very small?**

The agents become "short-sighted." Since they can only see what's right next to them, they mostly just try not to crash into each other, causing the big flock to shatter into tiny, isolated clusters.
**4. What happens when perception radius becomes very large?**

They try to connect with everyone at once. This usually causes the whole group to either collapse into a single, super-dense swarm or get trapped in a weird vibrating cycle of snapping together and pushing apart.

**5. What causes a flock to fragment?**

A flock breaks apart if the "separation" rule is turned up way too high, or if their vision radius shrinks too much. Basically, if they care more about dodging each other than flying together, the group shatters.

**6. How is Boids similar to Cellular Automata?**

They’re both classic examples of emergent behavior. In both, you get incredibly complex, self-organizing global patterns just by having independent units follow super simple local rules.

**7. How is Boids different from Neural Cellular Automata?**

Boids uses hardcoded, fixed rules. Neural Cellular Automata actually uses machine learning and neural networks to *learn* its local rules so it can grow and repair structures dynamically.

**8. What examples of emergent behavior exist in the real world?**

It's everywhere. In nature, you see it in ant colonies, bird flocks, and fish schools. For humans, things like traffic jams, economic markets, and even how entire societies form are all emergent behaviors that happen without a singular leader.
